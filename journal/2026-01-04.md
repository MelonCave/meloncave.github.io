**Domain I: The Structural and Functional Architecture of the Nervous System**

The foundational competency for any neuroscientific inquiry is a robust, three-dimensional mental model of the central nervous system (CNS). The citizen neuroscientist must move beyond static two-dimensional textbook representations to engage with dynamic, volumetric, and histological datasets. This domain establishes the spatial and structural scaffolding necessary to interpret functional data.

### **1.1 Comprehensive Neuroanatomical Atlases and Dissection Modules**

The study of gross neuroanatomy requires a tactile understanding of tissue properties and spatial relationships that is often lost in digital translation. However, recent advances in high-definition videography and interactive web modules have created virtual proxies that rival physical dissection.

**The University of British Columbia (UBC) Clinical Neuroanatomy** curriculum 1 stands as a primary pillar for this stage. Unlike resources that isolate structure from function, the UBC platform integrates clinical phenomenology with anatomical location. The "Stroke Model" and "Interactive Modules" allow the learner to simulate vascular occlusions—such as distinguishing between Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) and Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) infarcts—and observe the resulting functional deficits.1 This correlates directly with the **UBC Neuroanatomy Video Series**, which systematically dissects the CNS from the spinal cord to the cortex.2 The pedagogical value lies in its sequential complexity; learners progress from the gross morphology of the brainstem 2 to the intricate interconnectivity of the limbic system, establishing a "wiring diagram" mentality early in the curriculum.

Complementing this is the **University of Utah Neuroanatomy Video Lab**.3 The limitations of fixed tissue are well-known; formalin hardens the brain, distorting its natural consistency. The Utah collection, particularly the "Unfixed Brain" video 4, offers a rare view of the brain in its natural, gelatinous state. Dr. Suzanne Stensaas’s demonstrations provide essential insight into the vulnerability of the parenchyma to mechanical stress, a concept critical for understanding Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI).4 The series covers twenty-six specific modules, including deep dives into the **Cranial Nerves**, **The Ventricles**, and **The Meninges**, providing a level of detail usually reserved for medical students.4

For a systems-level perspective, **McGill University’s "The Brain from Top to Bottom"** offers an "Advanced" tier that bridges gross anatomy with evolutionary biology.5 This resource is critical for deconstructing persistent myths, such as the oversimplified "triune brain" theory. Instead, it presents a nuanced view of the **Evolutionary Layers of the Human Brain**, explaining how the neocortex integrates with, rather than suppresses, older limbic structures.5 The modules on **The Amygdala and Fear Circuitry** 5 connect these anatomical loci to behavioral outputs, serving as a primer for the affective neuroscience covered later in this report.

### **1.2 Multi-Modal and Genomic Atlases**

Once gross anatomy is mastered, the curriculum advances to the molecular and cellular definition of brain structures. The **Allen Institute for Brain Science** provides the definitive resources in this arena. The **Allen Human Brain Atlas** 6 is unique in its integration of genomic data with magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). It represents a multi-modal map where users can query specific gene expression profiles—an "all genes, all structures" RNA microarray survey—mapped to standard reference space.6 This allows the independent scholar to correlate structural definitions not just with shape, but with molecular identity.

Parallel to this, the **Allen Mouse Brain Connectivity Atlas** 7 offers a mesmerizingly detailed view of axonal projections. Using viral tracers, this resource maps the "connectome" at a mesoscopic scale, revealing the directionality and density of neural pathways that are invisible in human MRI. Understanding these projection maps is essential for interpreting the circuit diagrams found in computational models. The **Allen Cell Types Database** further refines this by characterizing neurons not just by location, but by their electrophysiological profiles and morphology 7, a dataset that underpins the computational modeling of single neurons.

**The Blue Brain Project Cell Atlas** (EPFL) complements the Allen resources by providing a comprehensive estimate of the number, position, and type of every neuron and glial cell in the mouse brain. This moves anatomy into the realm of quantitative data science, providing the boundary conditions necessary for biologically realistic simulations.

### **1.3 Neuropathology and Histological Analysis**

A sophisticated understanding of the "normal" brain requires a comparative analysis of pathology. **PathElective’s Introduction to Neuropathology** 8 serves as a virtual rotation in a pathology department. It provides high-resolution slides of normal neuroanatomy alongside pathological specimens, teaching the learner to identify the microscopic signatures of disease. The curriculum covers **Gross Normal Brain Anatomy** as a baseline before introducing the structural devastation of neurodegenerative diseases.8

The **University of Pittsburgh Neuropathology Slide Database** 9 functions as a virtual microscope. This tool allows for the examination of specific histological features, such as amyloid plaques, neurofibrillary tangles, and Lewy bodies, which are the hallmarks of Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s disease. Engaging with these raw visual data points 9 demystifies the diagnosis of these conditions, moving the learner from abstract descriptions to concrete visual recognition.

Finally, the **Harvard Brain Tissue Resource Center (HBTRC)** provides atlases that link post-mortem tissue analysis with clinical histories. This connection is vital for understanding the heterogeneity of neurological presentation—how similar structural pathologies can lead to divergent clinical outcomes.

**Domain II: Medical Neuroscience and Clinical Neurophysiology**

Moving beyond structure, this domain focuses on the dynamic function of the nervous system and its clinical manifestations. The citizen neuroscientist must grapple with the electrical language of the brain—from the ionic currents of single cells to the oscillatory dynamics of large-scale networks—and understand how these mechanisms are altered in disease states.

### **2.1 Systems Neuroscience and Bioelectricity**

The rigor of this curriculum is anchored by **Harvard Medical School’s HMX Fundamentals of Neuroscience**.10 This series provides a biophysical foundation, starting with **"The Electrical Properties of the Neuron"**.11 It is insufficient to merely know *that* neurons fire; one must understand *how*. This course covers the Nernst potential, the Hodgkin-Huxley kinetics of ion channels, and the passive membrane properties that determine signal propagation.11 Uniquely, it includes "DIY Labs" 11, encouraging the replication of electrophysiological principles in a home environment, thereby reinforcing theoretical knowledge with experimental practice.

**MIT OpenCourseWare’s 9.01: Introduction to Neuroscience** 12 serves as the academic backbone. It provides the broad theoretical framework, connecting molecular biology to cognitive science. For a more clinically oriented approach, the **Stanford Online Medical Neuroscience Graduate Certificate** 13 and **University of Michigan’s Medical Neuroscience (NEU 841\)** 14 offer graduate-level depth. These courses focus on the "localization of lesions"—the deductive reasoning process used by neurologists to identify the site of injury based on functional deficits.13

Understanding the chemical modulation of these electrical circuits is covered by **MIT OCW 9.98: Neuropharmacology**.15 This resource is essential for grasping the mechanisms of synaptic transmission and the impact of exogenous substances. It details the molecular sites of action for psychiatric medications, analgesics, and drugs of abuse, providing a mechanistic explanation for altered states of consciousness and the treatment of mental health disorders.15

### **2.2 Clinical Neurophysiology: EEG and Epilepsy**

The interpretation of Electroencephalography (EEG) is a specialized skill that bridges physics and medicine. The **American Epilepsy Society (AES) EEG Essentials** 16 is the definitive resource for this competency. Designed for clinical fellows and researchers, it offers a modular curriculum on the recognition of normal vs. abnormal brain rhythms. It trains the eye to distinguish between epileptiform discharges and artifacts, a skill that is increasingly relevant as consumer-grade EEG devices become available to the public.16

Supplementing this is the **University of Michigan NeuroLogic Program**, specifically the **EEG Tutorial**.17 This interactive tool utilizes case-based learning (51 distinct cases) to teach the principles of localization. It challenges the learner to correlate surface EEG findings with underlying cortical generators.17

For those interested in the critical care environment, the **Moberg CNS Bedside Monitoring Course** 18 introduces the complexities of multimodal monitoring. It explains how EEG data is integrated with Intracranial Pressure (ICP) and other physiological metrics to manage acute brain injury. This is complemented by the **Neonatal EEG** course 18, which offers insight into the developmental trajectory of brain rhythms, highlighting how the immature brain’s electrical signature differs from the adult.

The **American Clinical Neurophysiology Society (ACNS)** 19 provides high-level webinars on evoked potentials and intraoperative monitoring. These resources 19 are vital for understanding the functional integrity of sensory and motor pathways during surgical interventions.

### **2.3 Neurology and Neurovascular Disorders**

To ground physiological knowledge in clinical reality, the **University of Chicago Neurology Board Review** resources 21 provide expert-led overviews of neurovascular emergencies. Lectures by Dr. Issam Awad and colleagues dissect the pathophysiology of **Acute Stroke**, **Aneurysms**, and **Arteriovenous Malformations (AVMs)**.21 These resources explain the "time is brain" paradigm, detailing the cascade of ischemic injury and the window for therapeutic intervention.

**McGill University’s Neurology & Neurosurgery** resources 22 allow the learner to follow the educational track of medical residents. Accessing materials related to **Fellowships in Cognitive Neurology and Epilepsy** 23 provides exposure to complex case studies and the latest treatment protocols for refractory conditions.

In the realm of neurodegeneration, the **Wicking Dementia Research and Education Centre** (University of Tasmania) offers the **"Understanding Dementia" MOOC**.24 This is not a caregiver’s guide but a scientific exploration of the pathology of Alzheimer’s, Frontotemporal Dementia, and Lewy Body Disease. It presents the latest evidence on risk reduction, linking cardiovascular health and cognitive reserve to amyloid and tau pathology.25

Finally, the **International Neuroethics Society** 26 and **Society for Neuroscience (SfN) Neuronline** 27 provide the necessary meta-cognitive framework. Resources like **"Foundations of Rigorous Neuroscience Research"** 27 and webinars on the ethics of AI in brain health 28 ensure that the citizen neuroscientist engages with these powerful tools responsibly, understanding the societal implications of neurotechnology.

**Domain III: The Computational Turn – Simulation and Modeling**

The defining characteristic of modern neuroscience is its shift towards computation. The sheer volume of data generated by connectomics and electrophysiology necessitates algorithmic analysis and mathematical modeling. This domain empowers the learner to simulate the systems they have studied anatomically.

### **3.1 The Neuromatch Ecosystem and Deep Learning**

**Neuromatch Academy (NMA)** represents the gold standard in democratized computational neuroscience education. The **Computational Neuroscience** curriculum 29 is a rigorous, three-week intensive that moves from "What is a model?" to advanced topics like **Generalized Linear Models (GLMs)**, **Dimensionality Reduction**, and **Reinforcement Learning**. The syllabus is built on Python-based Jupyter notebooks, ensuring that learners do not just read about models but execute them code-line by code-line.

Parallel to this is the **NMA Deep Learning** course.30 As Artificial Intelligence and neuroscience converge, understanding **Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)** and **Generative Models** (VAEs, GANs) is essential. The course focuses on "Deep Learning Thinking"—understanding the cost functions and optimization landscapes that drive learning in both artificial and biological agents.31 It covers **Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)**, which are particularly relevant for modeling the temporal dynamics of brain circuits.

### **3.2 Brain Simulation Platforms: From Ion Channels to Whole Brains**

For simulation at the cellular level, the **NEURON Simulation Environment** remains the industry standard.32 It allows for the precise modeling of membrane voltage dynamics based on the density and kinetics of specific ion channels. The **Open Source Brain (OSB)** initiative 33 complements this by providing a repository of standardized models. Its tutorials on **Hodgkin-Huxley Kinetics** and **Multicompartmental Cell Modeling** 33 allow the user to visualize how changes in channel conductance alter action potential shape and propagation.

At the network level, **The Virtual Brain (TVB)** platform 34 enables the simulation of whole-brain dynamics. TVB integrates structural connectivity data (DTI) to simulate macroscopic activity (EEG/fMRI). Its **"Epilepsy Modeling"** tutorial 34 is particularly instructive, demonstrating how local hyperexcitability can propagate through the connectome to generate seizure activity. This links back directly to the clinical EEG knowledge gained in Domain II.

**Brian 2** 35 offers a more Pythonic entry point for Spiking Neural Network (SNN) simulation. Its intuitive syntax makes it ideal for testing hypotheses regarding coding and plasticity before moving to heavier environments. **ModelDB** 36, hosted by Yale, serves as the library for these efforts. It is a repository of published code; the citizen neuroscientist’s task is to download these models and replicate the figures from seminal papers, a practice that ensures true comprehension of the underlying mathematics.

**NeuroMorpho.Org** 37 provides the "hardware" for these simulations. It is a curated inventory of digitally reconstructed neurons. Users can download 3D morphological files (SWC format) to run simulations on realistic dendritic trees 38, moving beyond the oversimplified "point neuron" models often used in basic machine learning.

### **3.3 Neuroinformatics and Data Standards**

Access to data is no longer the bottleneck; organization is. **Neurodata Without Borders (NWB)** 39 is the emerging ISO standard for neurophysiology data. Understanding the NWB format is the prerequisite for using modern archives like the **DANDI Archive**.40 DANDI hosts petabytes of intracellular and extracellular recordings, optophysiology, and behavioral data. The **NWB "Introduction" tutorials** 39 teach the user how to navigate these massive HDF5 files, effectively unlocking the output of billion-dollar research initiatives for personal analysis.

**EBRAINS** 41, the infrastructure of the Human Brain Project, offers European-centric resources, including the **SpiNNaker** neuromorphic computing platform and the **QUINT** workflow for atlas-based quantification. This highlights the global nature of the field and provides alternative tools for simulation and analysis.

**ReproNim (Reproducible Neuroimaging)** 42 addresses the replication crisis head-on. Its curriculum teaches the use of **Docker** and **Singularity** containers, ensuring that analysis environments are stable and shareable. Coupled with the **Open Science Framework (OSF)** tutorials 43 on pre-registration and version control, these resources instill the discipline required for rigorous scientific practice.

**BrainIAK (Brain Imaging Analysis Kit)** 44, developed by Princeton and Yale, pushes the boundaries of fMRI analysis. It moves beyond standard subtraction methods to **Full Correlation Matrix Analysis (FCMA)** and **Real-Time fMRI**. The tutorials cover **Multivariate Pattern Analysis (MVPA)** and **Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA)** 45, techniques that decode *information content* rather than just activation levels.

**Domain IV: Neuroimaging Analysis and Data Science**

This domain focuses on the "read-write" operations of brain imaging. It empowers the learner to take raw magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) or diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) data and transform it into statistical maps of brain structure and function.

### **4.1 The Major Analysis Pipelines**

**FSL (FMRIB Software Library)** is a cornerstone of this domain. The **FSL Course** 46 provides a comprehensive education in fMRI and DTI analysis. It covers the **General Linear Model (GLM)** for task-based fMRI and **MELODIC** for Independent Component Analysis (ICA), a technique essential for isolating resting-state networks.47 The diffusion module introduces **Tract-Based Spatial Statistics (TBSS)**, allowing for the quantitative analysis of white matter integrity.47

**FreeSurfer**, developed by Harvard/MGH, is the standard for cortical surface reconstruction. The **FreeSurfer Course** 48 guides the user through the recon-all pipeline, which mathematically unfolds the cortex to measure thickness, curvature, and surface area. Crucially, it includes modules on **Longitudinal Analysis** 49, enabling the measurement of structural changes within the same subject over time—a key method in aging and neurodevelopmental research.

**SPM (Statistical Parametric Mapping)** 50 offers the statistical bedrock. While FSL and FreeSurfer are powerful, SPM focuses on the **Random Field Theory** that underpins the validity of neuroimaging statistics. Understanding the "mass-univariate" approach is critical for interpreting the "blobs" of activation seen in scientific papers.

**Nipype (Neuroimaging in Python)** 51 is the meta-tool that binds these packages together. It is a workflow engine that allows the user to mix and match the best tools from FSL, SPM, and FreeSurfer into a single, reproducible pipeline. The **Nipype Tutorial** 51 is essential for modern, automated neuroimaging analysis, facilitating the handling of large datasets like the **HCP Young Adult** release.52

### **4.2 Electrophysiology and Advanced Imaging**

**MNE-Python** 53 is the open-source industry standard for EEG and MEG analysis. Its tutorials cover the entire pipeline from **Epoching** continuous data to **Source Localization**.54 The latter is the mathematical inversion process used to estimate the cortical generators of scalp-recorded signals, linking the electrical data back to the anatomical models from Domain I.

**Andy’s Brain Book** 55 serves as the practical field guide. Created by Andy Jahn, it bridges the gap between dense technical documentation and day-to-day analysis, offering step-by-step walkthroughs for **AFNI**, **FSL**, and **SPM**.55 It demystifies concepts like "Cluster Correction" and "Region of Interest (ROI) Analysis."

**The Human Connectome Project (HCP) Course Materials** 52 represent the cutting edge. They train users in the **Connectome Workbench**, a tool designed to handle the high-resolution, multi-modal data of the HCP. This includes the "grayordinate" file formats that respect the geometry of the cortical sheet, offering superior spatial fidelity compared to traditional volumetric approaches.56

**BioImage Suite Web** and **NeuroDesk** 57 lower the barrier to entry by providing browser-based or containerized analysis environments. **NeuroDesk** is particularly valuable as it packages almost all major neuroimaging software into a Docker container, eliminating the often-prohibitive complexity of software installation and dependency management.57

**Domain V: Contemplative Science, Psychophysiology, and Interoception**

The final domain closes the loop, applying the rigorous scientific understanding of the nervous system to the subjective experience of the self. This is not "wellness" but **applied psychophysiology**—the use of specific, validated protocols to modulate the autonomic nervous system and refine interoceptive accuracy.

### **5.1 Clinical Mindfulness and Cognitive Regulation**

**UCSD’s Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) Teacher Training** 58 sets the clinical standard. This curriculum dissects the 8-week MBSR protocol, originally developed by Jon Kabat-Zinn. It does not treat mindfulness as a relaxation technique but as a regulatory intervention for the **Default Mode Network (DMN)** and stress reactivity. The training emphasizes the etiology of stress and the physiological mechanisms of "turning towards" difficult interoceptive signals.

**Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention (MBRP)** 59 adapts these principles for addiction. The professional training integrates Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) with mindfulness to target the neural circuitry of craving. It teaches "urge surfing"—a technique to decouple the sensation of craving from the behavioral response of consumption, effectively retraining the brain’s reward prediction error signal.60

**The Nalanda Institute’s Contemplative Psychotherapy Program** 61 offers a multi-year integration of Tibetan Buddhist psychology with Western neuroscience. Modules on **"Wise Compassion"** and **"Embodied Wisdom"** 61 provide a deep theoretical framework for self-transformation, grounded in the neuroplasticity of social cognition networks.

**The Mind & Life Institute** provides the primary literature for this domain. The **Varela Symposium** archives 62 feature high-level dialogues between contemplative scholars (like the Dalai Lama) and neuroscientists (like Richard Davidson and Wolf Singer). These discussions explore **Neurophenomenology**—the rigorous mapping of first-person experience to third-person physiological data.62

**The Center for Healthy Minds** (University of Wisconsin-Madison) 63 and the **Oxford Mindfulness Centre** provide the empirical backing. Their work on the **Healthy Minds Program** focuses on four pillars: Awareness, Connection, Insight, and Purpose. These are not abstract values but trainable skills correlated with specific neural signatures.64

### **5.2 Autonomic Regulation and Breathwork Protocols**

The citizen neuroscientist must understand the mechanics of the "physiological lever" that is the breath. **Heart Rate Variability (HRV) Biofeedback** is the quantitative cornerstone. The **BCIA Certification** resources 65 and the **Resonant Frequency Breathing** protocol (Lehrer & Gevirtz) 66 provide the scientific method for maximizing baroreflex gain. By breathing at an individual’s resonant frequency (typically \~5.5-6 breaths/min), one can phase-lock respiration and heart rate, inducing high-amplitude HRV and autonomic balance.67

**The Physiological Sigh**, popularized by the Huberman Lab and researched by David Spiegel’s team at Stanford, offers a distinct mechanism.68 This protocol—two inhales followed by a long exhale—mechanically reinflates collapsed alveoli and triggers a rapid vagal outflow. Clinical trials 69 demonstrate its superiority over box breathing for acute stress reduction and mood improvement.70

**Tummo Meditation (g-Tummo)** represents an extreme of autonomic control. Research by Kozhevnikov et al. 71 distinguishes between the "Forceful Breath" (Vase Breathing), which generates thermogenesis via sympathetic activation, and the "Gentle Breath," which maintains it. This deconstructs a mystical practice into a physiological protocol involving brown adipose tissue activation and cognitive visualization.72

**The Polyvagal Institute’s Foundations Course** 73 translates Stephen Porges’ theory into practice. It maps the autonomic hierarchy—from the primitive **Dorsal Vagal** shut-down to the mobilized **Sympathetic** state, and finally to the **Ventral Vagal** social engagement system. Tools for "autonomic mapping" allow the learner to identify their physiological state and apply the appropriate "neural exercise" to shift it.73

**Coherent Breathing** (Stephen Elliott) 74 and the **Benson-Henry Institute’s Relaxation Response** 75 offer foundational protocols for inducing the "trophotropic" (restorative) state. These practices reduce metabolic demand and lower the noise floor of the nervous system, facilitating the deeper signal detection required for advanced meditation.
