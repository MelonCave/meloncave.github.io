The strategy for exercise is not that complicated, but it's largely a matter of **discipline** ... a person must find ways to increase the amount and intensity of exercise one gets every day -- MOSTLY, this is just a matter of *just doing it* or "getting one's reps* in, but one can spice it up by trying new techniques and trends such as [*Norwegian 4x4 workout*](https://lifehacker.com/health/what-is-norwegian-4x4-workout) ... but exercise is about finding and then sticking with what works, so that one continues to develop one's discipline every day ... in theory, a person could overdo the discipline of increasing levels of exercise -- but PRACTICALLY, most people just will not CONSISTENTLY exercise enough or CONSISTENTLY with enough intensity -- almost noboby exercises too much on a CONSISTENT basis, especially not if they have a well-rounded discipline with respect to all aspects of their health ... sure, that kind of virtuous extremism might theoretically be possible -- but, CONSISTENTLY overdoing it is RARE ... even the best of the best of the best athletes, performers or operators know that they probably could exercise with greater intensity, efficacy and focus, CONSISTENTLY ensuring that each rep is optimal ... thus, the real value of exercise is not really in the physiological improvements, but rather the value is largely found as a matter of **discipline**.

Nutrition is similarly not really all THAT complicated ... but, nutrition and diet are also very much a matter of discipline and applied knowledge ... a simple, but necessary discipline to follow is to to completely AVOID the standard American diet ... AVOID sugar and anything that tastes sweet and encourages sugar cravings; avoiding things that induce cravings extends to ultra-processed and processed carbs and starches, eg breads, pastas, snacks, which are rapidly converted to glucose. The very same principle applies to easily digested news or sports or memes or any other form of entertainment/distraction that one craves and over-ingests ... ... AVOID sugar and anything that tastes sweet and encourages cravings for sweet, rapidly-digested items.  MOSTLY, dietary discipline is a matter of the cravings you AVOID ... thus, dietary discipline is not that complicated ... if you find yourself craving a dopamine hit or some form of *sweet junk*, then go without it, don't ingest it, make it more difficult to obtain, so that you won't be tempted to even think about getting a dopamine fix -- cut yourself some slack if you *fall off the wagon* but self-control is about the discipline of tapering off and driving cravings to zero.
